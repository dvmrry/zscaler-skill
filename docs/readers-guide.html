Chapter I

The castle and the moat, and why it stopped working

For thirty years, network security looked like medieval fortification. Then the apps left the castle — and so did the people.

There is a useful, almost too‑neat metaphor that everyone in security uses, and it is worth starting with because it explains the shape of every product that comes after. The metaphor is the castle and the moat. A company built a strong outer wall — a firewall, a VPN concentrator, a demilitarised zone — and once you were inside the wall, you were trusted. Applications inside assumed the requester was authenticated by virtue of being on the inside. The network was the security boundary.

This worked beautifully for a particular set of conditions: everyone worked in offices, applications lived in corporate data centres, and laptops never left the building. It was a comfortable model. It rewarded investment in perimeter: thicker walls, deeper moats, fewer drawbridges.

And then four things happened in the same decade.

Four things that broke the perimeter

Applications moved to SaaS. Salesforce, Workday, Office 365, Slack — the most important business systems no longer lived inside any wall. There was no perimeter to put them behind, because the vendor's perimeter was the vendor's problem.

Users went mobile. Laptops on hotel Wi‑Fi shouldn't be trusted automatically — but a VPN that pretends the laptop is "inside the office" implies exactly that.

Insider threats became prominent. One compromised user inside the perimeter, intentionally or otherwise, meant full access. The wall was strong against outsiders and useless against credentials phished out of an employee.

And lateral movement became the standard attack pattern. Modern intrusions don't break the wall; they enter through a single weak machine — a contractor's laptop, an unpatched server — and then pivot through the trusted internal network, which is, by design, friendly.

An observation The perimeter never actually disappeared. What disappeared was the singular perimeter. Zero trust is not the absence of walls — it's walls at every door instead of one wall around the kingdom.

By the late 2010s, the castle‑and‑moat model was producing an industry of well‑armoured organisations being thoroughly compromised through their own front gates. Something had to give. What gave was the assumption that the network is the security boundary.

Chapter II

Zero trust, in three sentences and three principles

A philosophy more than an architecture. But every Zscaler product is a coordinated attempt to implement it.

The phrase is unfortunate. Zero trust sounds like an absence; it is in fact a presence — the presence of explicit verification at every hop, instead of inherited trust from a prior one. Three sentences capture the whole idea.

Trust nothing implicitly. Verify every connection. Authorise per resource, never per network. — The zero‑trust posture, compressed

From those three sentences fall three operating principles, which the rest of this guide depends on:

Verify explicitly

Every connection authenticates and authorises against the user's identity, the device's posture, and the request's context. There is no shortcut for being on the inside of a firewall. Identity is asked at the door of every room, not just the front gate.

Apply least privilege

A user gets access to specific applications they are authorised for — not "the network." Knowing that someone can reach a jump host does not imply they can ping every other server in the data centre. The unit of access is the application, not the subnet.

Assume breach

Build the architecture as though the attacker is already somewhere inside. Limit blast radius through segmentation. Detect lateral movement. Rotate credentials. Record sessions. Make the inside as suspicious as the outside, because in any honest accounting, it already is.

What zero trust is not

It is not a single product. It is an architecture; vendors implement it differently. It is not the literal absence of trust — it is verified trust per connection. It is not impossible to bypass; misconfigured policies have escape hatches, and the implementation always matters more than the label. And it is not just for remote work — zero trust applies inside the office, inside the data centre, between cloud workloads. Remote access without a VPN is one application of zero trust. It is not the whole of it.

Chapter III

The Zero Trust Exchange — what is actually running

Stripped of marketing, Zscaler is a globally distributed proxy fleet sitting in front of every connection your users and workloads make. Here is what is in the cloud.

Zscaler markets its platform as the Zero Trust Exchange, and the name does real work. Exchange is the right word: every connection from a Zscaler‑protected user, device, or workload flows through the cloud first, gets inspected and policy‑applied, and is then forwarded on to its destination. It is not a tunnel from A to B with security bolted on; it is an explicit, mediated handover. Traffic enters Zscaler, Zscaler decides what to do with it, and only then does it leave.

The Exchange is the platform layer on which every product sits. It replaces, conceptually, three older categories of equipment:

Web gateways and firewalls, for outbound traffic to the internet. VPN concentrators, for inbound access from users to private apps. And inline appliances — the boxes that did inspection, filtering, and DLP in a corporate data centre. All three move out of the customer's racks and into Zscaler's data centres.

The control plane and the data plane

The cloud splits cleanly into two halves, which is worth holding onto because most operational questions resolve to one or the other.

The Central Authority is the control plane — the brain. It holds the authoritative copy of every customer's configuration and policy. It distributes that policy, plus threat‑intelligence and URL‑classification feeds, down to the data plane. It accepts admin API traffic. It monitors the health of the rest of the cloud. It does not — this is important — process customer traffic. Customer packets never pass through the Central Authority.

The Service Edges are the data plane — the muscle. They are the proxies that customer traffic actually flows through. They terminate the customer's tunnel, inspect the traffic, apply policy, and forward it on. They come in three form factors with the same software stack: Public (operated by Zscaler, multi‑tenant, the default), Private (Zscaler‑managed hardware on the customer's premises), and Virtual (customer‑operated VMs).

A useful split Control plane questions ("why isn't my policy taking effect?") and data plane questions ("why is this connection slow?") almost never have the same answer. Knowing which half you are looking at is the start of every diagnosis.

The fact that does the most explanatory work

It is worth stating once, plainly, because it explains nearly everything that follows: ZIA and ZPA are separate services running on separate multi‑tenant infrastructure. They share the Exchange brand, but a tenant who buys both gets two Central Authorities, two sets of Service Edges, two log streams. They are linked through the shared identity layer and the shared certificate authority — but they are not one service.

This is the cause of a great deal of operator confusion, and the source of most of the cross‑product subtleties documented later. The two halves of Zscaler — outbound to the internet and inbound to private apps — were built independently, and the seams are real.

This is also why this guide treats them as two separate chapters. They are.

Chapter IV

ZIA — outbound traffic, inspected at the edge

The first half of the Exchange. A cloud‑hosted secure web gateway, sitting between every user and the internet they are trying to reach.

If a user opens a browser and types in a URL, ZIA is the product that decides what happens next. It is, in the unromantic technical sense, a cloud‑hosted proxy. Every web request from a Zscaler‑protected device flows to a Public Service Edge — the nearest one, geographically — and that Service Edge is given the chance to inspect, modify, allow, block, or redirect the request before any of it reaches the actual internet.

The product's job is to replace what used to be a stack of appliances in a corporate data centre with a single, globally distributed inspection point. The boxes it replaces are familiar: secure web gateways, URL filters, SSL inspection appliances, sandbox detonation boxes, DLP appliances, content‑inspection appliances, IPS sensors. ZIA collapses them into modules running on the same edge.

What is happening inside an inspection

For an inspected HTTPS request, the Service Edge runs an ordered set of checks. The order matters; precedence is the source of most operator surprise. In rough sequence:

Forwarding & firewall

Layer‑3/4 firewall rules, NAT, DNS control. Network‑app identification fires here. A blocked IP never reaches the web modules.

SSL inspection

The pre‑decrypt pass. URL filtering and cloud‑app control evaluate against the SNI and IP, before any decryption.

Decryption

If the policy says inspect, the connection is decrypted. The Service Edge mints its own certificate to the client and proxies the upstream TLS session itself.

URL filtering & cloud‑app control, again

The post‑decrypt pass. The full URL — path, parameters — is now visible. Most categorisation decisions are finalised here.

Content inspection

DLP scans for sensitive data leaving. Sandbox detonates suspicious files. Malware and ATP modules examine payload reputation. File‑type controls enforce upload and download policy.

Forward upstream

The request is re‑encrypted and sent on to its destination. The response flows back through the same set of modules in reverse.

A common misconception "URL filtering happens before SSL inspection." It does — but it also happens after. Inspected HTTPS is evaluated twice, once on the SNI and once on the full URL. A rule that fires on one pass may not fire on the other. This is the single most useful thing to remember when a URL filter result surprises you.

Why this is the boring, good architecture

Putting this work in the cloud is unglamorous, and that is its strength. The same policy applies whether the user is in an office, a hotel, or a coffee shop — the perimeter follows the user. The threat‑intel feeds update once and propagate to every Service Edge globally. The customer never patches a sandbox box at 2 a.m. The model is monotonous and that is the point: the same inspection, every time, everywhere.

The data plane is hardened in unsurprising ways. The TCP stack runs in user mode for multi‑tenant isolation. Customer traffic never leaves the Service Edge — it is not forwarded to another Zscaler component. Nothing is written to disk. Logs are compressed, tokenised, and exported over TLS to a separate log infrastructure (the Nanolog cluster) that does not see the traffic itself.

Where ZIA ends

ZIA's remit ends at the public internet. It is the product for "the user is reaching out." The moment the destination is a private application — something inside a customer's data centre, or an internal AWS VPC — the question changes. A different product picks it up. That is the next chapter.

Chapter V

ZPA — inbound access, without a network

The second half of the Exchange. The product that is replacing the corporate VPN — and not by being a better VPN.

A VPN's defining property is that it joins your laptop to a network. Once you are on, you are on — you can reach every IP that has been routed across the link, whether you have any business reaching it or not. This is convenient. It is also, after thirty years of phished credentials and lateral movement, the single largest source of preventable corporate intrusions.

ZPA's pitch is simple and almost philosophically different: do not put the user on a network at all. Connect them, instead, only to the specific applications they are authorised to reach — one application at a time, each one its own policy decision, none of them implying access to anything else.

How that is actually done

The mechanism is more elegant than it sounds. A small piece of software runs inside the customer's network — the App Connector. It does not accept incoming connections; instead, it dials outbound to the Zscaler cloud and holds that connection open. There is no inbound port to expose, no firewall hole to drill, no public endpoint for an attacker to find.

When a user wants to reach an internal application, the request goes to ZPA. ZPA evaluates the access policy: is this user, on this device, in this posture, allowed to reach this application right now? If yes, ZPA stitches together two halves of an end‑to‑end channel — the user's tunnel from their device, and the App Connector's tunnel from inside the network — and the application traffic flows through both. If no, the user does not even learn that the application exists.

The application's real IP address is never returned to the user's device. DNS resolves to a synthetic IP that exists only inside ZPA's tunnel. From the network's perspective, the application is not reachable at all; from the user's perspective, the application is reachable on demand. Both are simultaneously true.

The user is never on the network. The network is never reachable. Access exists only at the moment of policy approval, for the duration of one application session. — ZPA in one paragraph

What gets evaluated, and when

The access policy is the heart of the product. For every connection request, ZPA evaluates a tuple — identity, device posture, location, time of day, the application being reached — and produces an allow or deny. Identity comes from the upstream identity provider, federated through ZIdentity. Posture comes from the endpoint agent. Location and time are obvious. The application is the thing being asked for.

Layered on top of this access decision are two narrower layers. AppProtection is an inline web application firewall: even authorised traffic to an authorised app is examined for application‑layer attacks — OWASP categories, attempts on Active Directory protocols, web‑shell signatures. Authentication, in zero‑trust thinking, is not unconditional trust. Privileged Remote Access (PRA) extends this further to the most sensitive sessions — RDP, SSH, VNC — adding approval workflows, credential pooling, and full session recording. The principle is the same: even administrators do not get implicit trust.

The architectural difference from ZIA

ZPA is built on the same Service Edge fabric as ZIA, but with two structurally different decisions worth flagging.

The Central Authority for ZPA is active‑active — geographically distributed nodes all servicing requests in parallel. ZIA's CA is active‑passive, one writer at a time. This shows up operationally as faster propagation in ZPA than in ZIA: configuration changes in ZPA take effect on write, with no separate activation step. ZIA still has a staging gate.

And the data path is genuinely end‑to‑end through the cloud. ZIA inspects, then forwards to the public internet. ZPA stitches a tunnel from the user's device, through a Service Edge, through an App Connector, to the application — the entire path is mediated by Zscaler. The application is, in the most literal sense, never exposed to the network the user is on.

Chapter VI

A single request, traced end to end

It helps to watch one request travel the whole pipeline. The pieces fit together more easily once you have seen them in motion.

Imagine an employee on a corporate laptop, at home, opening a browser and typing in the URL of an internal HR application — something that lives inside the company's data centre, behind no public DNS, on no routable address. What follows is what happens, in order, between the keystroke and the page load.

Identity is established

The endpoint agent on the laptop has already authenticated the user against the identity layer. A token is in hand. The user is, formally, who they claim to be.

Device posture is taken

The agent inspects the device — disk encryption on, antivirus running, OS patch level current — and produces a posture profile that will travel with the request.

DNS returns a synthetic address

The browser asks for the HR app's hostname. DNS, intercepted locally by the agent, returns a synthetic IP that exists only inside the ZPA tunnel — not the application's real address.

The connection enters the Exchange

The browser opens a connection to the synthetic IP. The agent routes that connection through the user tunnel to the nearest Service Edge.

Access policy evaluates

The Service Edge checks: this identity, this device posture, this location, this time of day, this application. Allow or deny. If deny, the connection terminates here and the user never learns the application was real.

AppProtection inspects

If allowed, the application‑layer firewall examines the request for attack patterns — OWASP categories, protocol abuse, anomalies. The traffic is passed, blocked, or flagged.

The far end of the tunnel opens

The Service Edge stitches its connection to the App Connector inside the company's data centre. The App Connector — which has been holding an outbound connection open the whole time — accepts the request.

The App Connector reaches the app

Inside the data centre, the App Connector makes a normal connection to the actual HR application's real IP. The response begins to flow back through the same path, in reverse.

The session is recorded

Every step has emitted a log line. They flow to the streaming layer, into the customer's SIEM, where they will be available for audit, alerting, and correlation.

Each of those nine steps is a verification gate. None of them assume trust based on the previous one. The user authenticated in step one is checked again against device posture in step five. The traffic that passed access policy in step five is inspected again at the application layer in step six. Even at the far end, the App Connector does not trust the request — it simply forwards it, having no way to act on it independently.

This is what zero trust looks like in motion. Not a single wall. Not a single decision. Many small verifications, one after another, each one cheap, each one mandatory, each one logged.

A short glossary

ZTE
Zero Trust Exchange — Zscaler's name for the unified cloud platform. Not a product itself; the layer all products live on.
ZIA
Internet & SaaS. The outbound half — proxy and inspection for user‑to‑internet traffic.
ZPA
Private Access. The inbound half — zero‑trust application access, the VPN replacement.
ZCC
Client Connector. The endpoint agent that decides where a device's traffic goes.
Service Edge
A data‑plane proxy in the Zscaler cloud. Public, Private, or Virtual — same software, three places it can run.
Central Authority
The control‑plane brain. Holds policy and configuration; never sees customer traffic.
App Connector
The piece of software inside a customer's environment that ZPA reaches private apps through. Always dials outbound.
Synthetic IP
The address a ZPA‑protected app appears to live at, on the user's device. Only meaningful inside the tunnel.
